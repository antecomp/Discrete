\subsubsection{Terminology}
\begin{addmargin}[2em]{2em}
	$ A \land B $ - \underline{conjuction} of \underline{conjuncts} $A$ and $B$ \\
	$ A \lor B $ - \underline{disjunction} of \underline{disjuncts} $A$ and $B$ \\
	$ A \implies B $ - $A$ is the \underline{hypothezsis\tiny{/antecedant}} and $B$ is the \underline{conclusion\tiny{/consequence}}  
\end{addmargin}

\subsubsection{Satifiability, Tautology, Contradiction}
\begin{itemize}[label={}, leftmargin=*]
	\item A proposition is \underline{satisfiable} if it is true for
	\sidenote[You don't need a whole truth table for this, just look for one!]\emph{at least one} 
	combination of boolean values.
	\item \underline{A Boolean Satisfiablity Problem (SAT)} is checking for satifiability in a propositional logic formula.
	\item \underline{A Tautology} is a proposition that is \underline{always true} \\ \hspace*{5mm} \exheader[ex] $ A \lor \neg A$
	\item \underline{A contradiction} is a proposition that is always false. \\ \hspace*{5mm} \exheader[ex] $ A \land \neg A$
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Truth Tables}
\bigbreak
Truth Tables are used for determining all the possible outputs of a complex compound propostion.
\\
\horizline
\\
\underline{The Columns} Are for the prepositions,
\sidenote[The intrmt' prepositions are optional steps to make solving easier, use as needed.]intermediate compound prepositions and the whole compound preposition.

\underline{The Rows} Are to contian the different sets of possible truth values for each proposition. You will have $2^p$ rows where $p$ is the number of propositions (then +1 for the header).

\horizline \\
\faWarning \space The connectives in a compound propositional logic problem follow an order of precedence (the PEMDAS of logic) in the following order; \\
$ \neg $ , $ \land $ , $ \lor $ , $ \implies $ , $ \iff $
